\begin{frame}{What is a Monopoly?}
    \begin{block}{A monopoly is...}
        A firm that is the sole seller of a product without any close substitutes

        \vspace{2mm}

        $\to$ Caused by barriers to entry in the market
    \end{block}
    Examples of monopolies include:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item local utilities
        \item local internet providers
        \item railroads
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{Barriers to entry}
What are barriers to entry?

\vspace{2mm}

\begin{block}{Monopoly resources:}
    A key resource required for production is owned by a single firm
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Government Regulations:}
    The government gives a single firm the exclusive right to produce some good or service
\end{block}

\begin{block}{The production process:}
    A single firm can produce output at a lower cost than can a larger number of firms $\to$ \textbf{natural monopoly}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Monopoly vs. Competitive Firms}
    \begin{block}{Key difference...}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Firms in a competitive market are \textbf{price takers}
        \item Monopoly firms are \textbf{price setters}
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}

    \vspace{2mm}

    How does the demand curve for a monopoly curve differ from that of a competitive firm?

\pause
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Competitive firm: $MR = P^* \to $ firms demand curve is the $MR$ curve
        \item Monopoly firm: demand curve is equal to the market demand curve
        \begin{itemize}
            \item the market demand prevents monopolies from setting crazy prices - if they did that nobody would buy anything
        \end{itemize}
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Monopoly Revenue}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $AR = P$: true for both monopoly and competitive firms.
    \item $MR < P$: true only for monopoly firms.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Recall: $MR = P$ for competitive firms.
    \end{itemize}
    \item A monopoly’s total revenue is $TR = P \times Q$; the monopolist chooses $P$ and $Q$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{block}{There is a revenue tradeoff:}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{Output effect:} $\uparrow Q \implies \uparrow TR$ because more units are sold.
            \item \textbf{Price effect:} $\uparrow Q \implies \downarrow P$, which reduces $TR$.
        \end{itemize}

    $MR$ becomes negative when the price effect exceeds the output effect.
\end{block}
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{Profit maximization}
  \begin{block}{How does any firm choose which quantity to produce? }
    Set $MC = MR$!
  \end{block}
  \vspace{2mm}
  This is the same for competitive and monopoly firms... But:
  \begin{itemize}
      \item For a monopoly - $P > MR = MC$
      \item For a competitive firm - $P = MR = MC$
  \end{itemize}
  
  \vspace{3mm}

  Profit is still calculated the same way :
  \[\pi = (P - ATC)\times Q\]
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Social welfare}
    \begin{block}{What is the socially optimal $Q$?}
        $Q$ where $MC = $ the demand curve
    \end{block}

    Since the $MR$ curve is less than the $D$ curve when $Q>0 \implies$ the socially optimal $Q^{SE} > Q^M $, the monopolists profit maximizing $Q$. 

    \vspace{3mm}

    This means that when a monopoly firm produces at $Q^M$, there is some deadweight loss!
    \begin{itemize}
        \item DWL similar to taxes... what is the difference here?
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Price discrimination}
    When monopoly firms produce at $Q^M$, price is higher than the socially optimal level $\to$ DWL

            \item Why might a firm want to price discriminate?
            \item What are some examples of price discrimination?
            \item How would price discrimination affect social welfare?
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}

    \vspace{2mm}

    What is perfect price discrimination? \pause $\to$ Each person who values the good at more than the $MC$ of the firm buys the good and is charged exactly their willingness to pay
\end{frame}
