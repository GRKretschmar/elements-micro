\begin{frame}{Costs of Production}
    A firm aims to maximize their profit:
    \[\text{Profit} = \text{total revenue} - \text{total cost}\]
\begin{itemize}
    \item total revenue: \(TR = P \times Q\)
    \item total cost is the sum of all \textbf{implicit} and \textbf{explicit} costs of inputs to production
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Implicit}: input costs which \textit{do not} require monetary outlay from the firm
        \item  \textbf{Explicit}: input costs which \textit{do} require a monetary outlay from the firm
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Types of Profit}
    There are two types of profit we can consider:
    \begin{block}{Economic Profit:}
        total revenue less implicit \textit{and} explicit costs
    \end{block}

    \begin{block}{Accounting}
        total revenue less explicit costs \textit{only}
    \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Costs}
\begin{block}{Types of costs:}
    \begin{itemize}
    \item Fixed costs: costs that do not vary with Q
    \item Variable costs: costs that vary with Q
\end{itemize}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Measures of costs:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Average total cost (ATC): \(ATC = TC/Q\)
    \item Average fixed cost (AFC): \(AFC = FC/Q\)
    \item Average variable cost (AVC): \(AVC = VC/Q\)
    \item Marginal Cost: \(MC = \Delta TC/\Delta Q\)
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{What do costs look like?}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item We tend to assume (like in previous example) that the marginal product of labor is decreasing... Why?
        \item Average fixed cost falls as output rises because the same fixed cost is spread over more units.
        \item Average variable cost typically falls at first and then rises: initially, workers can specialize and use fixed inputs more effectively, but eventually diminishing marginal product sets in.
        \item Marginal cost also often has a U-shape because when marginal product is rising, each additional unit is relatively cheap to produce, but when marginal product falls, each additional unit becomes more costly.
        \item Average total cost is the sum of average fixed cost and average variable cost, so it often has a U-shape: it falls at first, then rises once the increase in AVC outweighs the continued decline in AFC.
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{A Final Piece of Intuition}

The marginal cost curve intersects average total cost at the minimum of ATC: if MC is below ATC, producing one more unit pulls the average down; if MC is above ATC, producing one more unit pushes the average up.

\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{Economies and Diseconomies of Scale}
    We can define the scale of production in the following 3 ways:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Economies of Scale: Long run ATC declines as Q increases
        \item Diseconomies of Scale: Long run ATC increases as Q increases
        \item Constant returns to scale: Long run ATC stays the same as Q changes
    \end{enumerate}
\end{frame}




\begin{frame}{Competitive markets}
    \begin{block}{Let's recall what we mean by "competitive market":}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Many buyers and sellers $\implies$ No single buyer or seller has influence over price
            \item The goods/services being sold are homogeneous
            \item Firms can freely enter/exit the market \textit{(this is new!)}
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}
    \begin{block}{What does this mean for firms?}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Firms are price takers
            \begin{itemize}
                \item $P$ remains constant for a firm at any level of $Q$ $\implies$ $MR = P$
            \end{itemize}
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Competitive Markets}
\textbf{    Firms in competitive markets want to maximize their profit.
}
    \vspace{5mm}

    Recall that profit is:
    \[\pi = TR - TC\]
    
    Total revenue (TR) is:
    \[TR = P\times Q\]

    \begin{block}{We also consider average and marginal revenue:}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Average revenue: \(AR = \frac{TR}{Q} = \frac{P\times Q}{Q} = P\)
            \item Marginal revenue: \(MR = \frac{\Delta TR}{\Delta Q}\) \quad ($=P$ in a competitive market)
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Optimal Choices in a competitive market}
    Firms will always maximize their profit when they choose to produce the $Q$ where $MR = MC$!
    \vspace{3mm}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item This is a direct result of the firms thinking on the margin:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item If $MR > MC$ the firm should increase $Q$
            \item If $MR < MC$ the firm should decrease $Q$
        \end{itemize}
        \item \textbf{** The only $Q$ where they do not want to make a change is the $Q$ where $MR = MC$ **}
    \end{itemize}

\vspace{5mm}

Because MC determines $Q$ for any $P$ (we look for $Q$ such that $MR = MC$ and in a competitive market $MR = P$), the MC curve is the supply curve for a \textit{competitive} firm
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Firm Shutdown}
    Importantly, a firm does not want to operate at a loss (negative profit)

    \vspace{4mm}

    In the short run, a firm decides whether to remain open or shut down \textit{for a period of time} (Not permanently!!!)

    \begin{block}{A firm should shutdown in the short run if:}
        \[P < AVC\]
        \begin{itemize}
            \item If the firm earns less per unit sold than the \textbf{variable cost} of producing that unit, they should shut down
            \item In the SR, there are fixed costs (rent, machines, etc.) that must be paid for whether or not the firms shuts down temporarily
            \begin{itemize}
                \item These costs are called \textbf{\textit{sunk costs,}} and we don't consider them in short term decisions 
            \end{itemize}
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Firm Exit}
    Importantly, a firm does not want to operate at a loss (negative profit)

    \vspace{4mm}

    In the long run, a firm decides whether to remain in the market or exit the market \textit{(Permanently!!!)}

    \begin{block}{A firm should exit the market in the long run if:}
        \[TR < TC \iff P < ATC\]
        \begin{itemize}
            \item If the firm earns less per unit sold than the \textit{total cost} of producing that unit, they should exit the market
            \item We consider fixed costs in the long run because we can end our lease on the building/sell the machines in the long run
            \begin{itemize}
                \item \textit{These costs are not sunk in the long run}
            \end{itemize}
            \item We can say that a firm should enter the market if the inequality is reversed
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Supply curves}
    The supply curve of a competitive firm is the portion of their MC curve where they remain in the market, which is different in the short and long run...

    \begin{block}{In the short run}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Supply is 0 when \(MC<AVC\) \quad ($MC = MR = P$)
            \item Supply curve is the portion of MC curve which lies above the AVC curve
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}

        \begin{block}{In the long run}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Supply is 0 when \(MC<ATC\)
            \item Supply curve is the portion of MC curve which lies above the ATC curve
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{How does shutdown/exit affect market equilibrium?}
    \begin{block}{In the short run...}
        Firms may shutdown $\implies$ we may be pushed away from equilibrium
    \end{block}

    \begin{block}{In the long run...}
        Firms may exit the market $\implies$ supply shifts left (less sellers)
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Drives prices up
            \item More firms enter the market because the price is higher
            \item Price drops back to the equilibrium level
        \end{itemize}

        \begin{itemize}
            \item Firms that remain in the market must be making zero economic profit ($MC = MR$)
            \item Entry/Exit only stops when $ATC = MR = MC = P$
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}
\end{frame}
